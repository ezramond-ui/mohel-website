A Guide for New Parents

The bris process, explained gently

If you're feeling a little nervous, that's completely natural — and you're in good hands. Here is a warm, honest walkthrough of what a brit milah is and what your family can expect.

What is a bris (brit milah)?

A brit milah — often simply called a bris — is the Jewish ceremony of covenant, marked by the ritual circumcision of a baby boy. The words mean “covenant of circumcision.” It is one of the oldest and most cherished traditions in Jewish life, connecting your son to generations of family and faith that came before him.

A mohel (pronounced MOY-el) is the trained specialist who performs the bris. A mohel combines religious knowledge with careful technique — someone who has performed the ceremony many times and does so with reverence, gentleness, and skill.

Why the eighth day?

Tradition calls for the bris to take place on the eighth day of the baby's life, counting the day of birth as day one. So a baby born on a Monday would have his bris the following Monday. The eighth day is observed even when it falls on Shabbat or a holiday.

Remarkably, this ancient timing aligns with modern medicine. By the eighth day, a newborn's natural blood-clotting factors — including vitamin K and healthy platelet levels — have fully developed, which helps keep bleeding to a minimum and supports a quick, smooth recovery.

A note on timing: Babies born in the evening, by C-section, or with any health considerations may need an adjusted date. Don't worry about calculating this yourself — reach out and I'll help you determine the correct day.

Who is typically involved?

Part of the beauty of a bris is sharing honors with the people you love. These roles are warm, meaningful, and easy — I'll explain each one and help you decide who does what.

  • Sandek — a great honor, often given to a grandfather or close relative, who holds the baby during the ceremony.
  • Kvatter & Kvatterin — a couple (often godparents) who carry the baby into the room.
  • Parents — who recite a blessing and, most movingly, announce their son's Hebrew name.
  • Family & friends — who gather to witness, celebrate, and share in the joy.

What happens during the ceremony

The ceremony itself is brief — usually just a few minutes — and is followed by blessings, the naming, and a festive meal. Here's the flow:

1

Welcoming

The baby is carried in and welcomed with the words “Baruch haba” — blessed is the one who arrives.

2

The circumcision

The bris itself is performed swiftly and gently — typically just five to ten seconds — with comfort measures for your baby throughout.

3

Blessings & naming

We recite blessings over wine and the covenant, and your son receives his Hebrew name.

4

Celebration

Mazel tov! The bris concludes with a festive meal (seudat mitzvah) shared with loved ones.

Is it safe? A word on comfort and medical practice

Your baby's safety and comfort come first, always. Because the procedure is so brief — only a few seconds — and uses a careful, time-tested technique, discomfort is kept to a minimum. No injections or drugs are needed, and a gentle topical numbing cream is available on request. Most babies settle within moments, and many sleep right through the celebration afterward.

I perform the bris using the traditional Mogen shield method — a shielded technique refined over generations that is quick, precise, and results in minimal bleeding and a fast, comfortable recovery.

  • Traditional Mogen shield technique — quick and precise
  • Sterile, single-use instruments
  • No injections or drugs; optional topical numbing on request
  • Careful pre-ceremony check to confirm your baby is ready
  • Clear written aftercare instructions and personal follow-up by phone
  • Coordination with your pediatrician whenever needed

If your baby isn't ready on day eight, that's okay. Health always comes first. If your pediatrician advises waiting — for jaundice, prematurity, or any reason — we simply reschedule for when your son is strong and ready.

How to prepare

There's very little you need to do — I bring everything required for the ceremony. Before the day, I'll send you a simple checklist. In general, it helps to have:

  • A sturdy table in a well-lit area
  • Two soft pillows or a cushion
  • Fresh diapers, wipes, and a change of clothes for the baby
  • Kosher wine or grape juice and a cup for the blessings
  • Optional: a tallit or special garment, if your family has one

Most of all, prepare to be present. This is a joyful milestone — try to soak it in.

Before & after: I'm with you the whole way

We'll connect before the bris — by phone or in person — so you know exactly what to expect and every question is answered. Afterward, I provide detailed aftercare guidance and check in personally. Healing is usually quick and uncomplicated, and I remain available to you.
